# Preface

## 1. A Word from the Editor

My grandfather, Vasiko Tkemaladze, was never a certified physician. His clinic was our Tbilisi home on a street where the morning air smelled of freshly baked *tonis puri* (Georgian bread) and fried beans. His instruments were a heavy cast-iron *kotsi* (cauldron) for *lobio* (bean dish), a wooden mortar for *adjika*, and endless patience. He could spend hours explaining why *sulguni* cheese must be aged in brine for at least forty days, and why the first thing to do with a chicken is to thoroughly bleed it. My grandmother, Lali Gegeshidze, complemented his knowledge with her wisdom of herbs: she knew when to gather *ekala* (young blackberry shoots) for a side dish to get the most magnesium, and how to properly ferment *jinjoli* (pickled flowers of the bladdernut tree) so they would become not just a spicy snack, but a living medicine for the gut.

In our family, food was never just fuel. It was a language spoken about health, longevity, and harmony. Around the common table, under the shade of the grapevine in the courtyard, rules were born that I, later as a dietitian, began to record and systematize. It was an empirical system, forged by generations, based on deep observation: which foods bring lightness, and after which ones the heart feels heavy; what to eat in winter to avoid illness, and in summer to preserve strength. It was wisdom inseparable from Georgian hospitality, where proper nutrition is not ascetic self-denial, but the art of choosing the best from nature's generous gifts and preparing it with love and knowledge.

Becoming a doctor, I encountered a paradox. On one hand, there was the priceless, intuitively correct family code of nutrition. On the other, there was the strict, logically impeccable system of therapeutic diets by Professor Manuil Isakovich Pevzner, which became the gold standard of Soviet and post-Soviet clinical dietetics. For a long time, these two worlds existed in parallel, unaware of each other. Pevzner, a genius of systematization, created his "tables" for patients in hospital wards, based on physiology and pathogenesis. My ancestors created their system for the healthy and those wishing to remain so, based on tradition and empiricism. And only after years of meticulous work did I begin to see astonishing points of intersection.

It turned out that the prohibition of pork and game in our family had the same basis as the restriction of refractory fats and extractive substances in liver diseases according to Pevzner. The recommendation to eat fruit only in the first half of the day echoed the principles of diets for carbohydrate metabolism disorders. And the strict taboo on commercial yeast and fresh milk proved to be a brilliant, albeit intuitive, way to prevent dysbiosis and autoimmune reactions, which were not yet known in the mid-20th century. This book is an attempt at synthesis. An attempt to build bridges between deep but fragmented folk wisdom and the powerful, yet sometimes overly rigid, clinical apparatus.

## 2. Two Parallel Universes: The Family Table and the Clinical Office

The Pevzner system, born in the scientific institutes of Moscow and Leningrad, is the dictatorship of diagnosis. It answers the question: "What can one eat with a stomach ulcer, pancreatitis, or gout?" Its strength lies in its universality, evidence base, and clarity of prescriptions. It is the language spoken by doctors across the post-Soviet space. "Table No. 5," "Table No. 9" — these numbers have become part of medical folklore.

The Tkemaladze-Gegeshidze system, born in the kitchen of a Tbilisi home, is the dictatorship of common sense and prevention. It answers the question: "What should one always eat to prevent ulcers, pancreatitis, or gout?" Its strength lies in its holistic nature, focus on long-term health, and inseparable connection with cultural context. It is a system where *bajhe* (walnut sauce) is not just a sauce, but a source of omega-3; where *tkemali* (sour plum sauce) is not just a sour sauce, but a fermented product regulating digestion; and where butter is the only permissible dairy fat, cleansed of harmful casein.

For many years, these systems did not intersect. Clinicians considered folk dietetic advice unserious. Keepers of traditions saw therapeutic diets as tasteless "hospital" food devoid of joy. My discovery was that they do not contradict but complement each other. The Pevzner system provides a map of the terrain with marked danger zones (disease exacerbations). My family's system offers a compass and rules for navigating this terrain to never enter those zones.

For example, Pevzner in "Table No. 1" (for ulcers) prohibits cabbage broth and raw vegetables. Our family protocol prohibits white cabbage in any form at all times, considering it a source of "concentrated poisonous substances" that irritate mucous membranes. Coincidence? No. It is a view of the same problem from different angles: clinical (when damage already exists) and preventive (to prevent damage). The same applies to choleretic products, salt, and methods of cooking meat.

This book is the result of many years of work deciphering, comparing, and synthesizing these two great systems. We are not canceling Pevzner. We propose overlaying his therapeutic "tables" with the basic, universal filter of the Tkemaladze-Gegeshidze system. This filter screens out products that are fundamental irritants and dysbiotics, regardless of diagnosis. And it is on this purified foundation that an individual therapeutic diet is built.

# Chapter 1. The Philosophy of Nutrition

_The System of Vasiko Tkemaladze and Lali Gegeshidze_

## 1.1. "90% of Health Depends on Food"

There are truths that do not need proof — they are tested by generations. In the Tkemaladze family, this truth was transmitted not in the form of lectures, but through the daily ritual of the table: what to cook, when to eat, what never to serve to a guest. Vasiko Tkemaladze and his wife Lali Gegeshidze were not dietitians in the academic sense. They were keepers of living knowledge — what in European medicine is called "traditional dietetics," and in the Caucasus was simply called "the right way of life."

When their grandson, Dr. Jaba Tkemaladze, began to systematize the family's nutritional principles in the context of clinical practice, he discovered something astonishing: most of them anticipated the findings of modern nutrition science by decades. The principle "the first broth — always discard" echoes modern data on the bioaccumulation of xenobiotics in meat. The prohibition on thermophilic yeast — with research on the gut microbiome. Fruit only in the first half of the day — with the circadian physiology of digestion.

But the main principle of the system is simple: **90% of health depends on what you eat.** The remaining 10% is sleep, movement, and mental state. Medicine treats symptoms. Nutrition — forms the soil on which disease either sprouts or does not.

This does not mean that proper nutrition replaces a doctor. It means that it makes the doctor less frequently needed.

---

## 1.2. The Principle of "Permitted / Moderate / Forbidden"

The Tkemaladze-Gegeshidze nutrition system is not built on calorie counting. It is built on the **qualitative differentiation of products** — into three categories that determine not energy value, but the impact on the body's internal environment.

**Desirable (Permitted)** — foods that nourish without burdening. They support acid-base balance, do not create excessive load on the liver, gallbladder, and intestines, and do not provoke chronic inflammation.

**Moderate** — foods that are acceptable in small quantities and with good health, but become problematic with abuse or in the presence of chronic diseases.

**Forbidden** — foods that should not be present on the table regardless of quantity. Not because they are "harmful in large doses" — they are harmful in any dose for the physiology described by this system.

It is important to understand: this is **not diet therapy in the Pevzner sense**. It is the basic philosophy of nutrition for a healthy and conditionally healthy person. When a specific disease arises — the Tkemaladze-Gegeshidze system becomes a *filter* through which Pevzner's recommendations are passed. The principles of this synthesis are discussed in Part III of the book.

---

## 1.3. The Three Immutable Principles of the System

At the foundation of the synthesis of Georgian and Soviet dietetic schools lie not just rules, but philosophical tenets that form a holistic medical approach. These are the three pillars supporting the logic of every subsequent protocol and recommendation.

**Principle 1. "You Cannot Do One Thing and Spoil More"**

Diet therapy is always a weighing of risks and benefits. Every intervention must be evaluated by its overall net effect. If eliminating one pathogenic factor leads to the emergence of another, more significant one — the strategy is flawed. Thus, thoughtlessly excluding an entire food group (e.g., dairy) under the pretext of hypothetical benefit for the microbiome can turn into a catastrophe if the resulting vacuum in the diet is filled by the person with cheap sausages and refined carbohydrates. We are not treating, but merely changing the form of the disease. Consequently, any dietary restriction must immediately be accompanied by the question: "And what compensates for this?" Balance is the highest priority.

**Principle 2. "Replace, Do Not Prohibit"**

Absolute prohibitions often demoralize the patient and impoverish their diet. It is much more productive to seek functional analogues. The task is to identify the valuable in the "forbidden" product and find a clean alternative for it without accompanying harm. If potato starch is unacceptable, but potassium is needed — we turn to dried apricots, pumpkin, and greens. With lactose or milk casein intolerance — we consider brine-aged cheeses, fermented products, small fish with bones as a source of calcium. This is not a sacrifice, but a creative process of building a new, safer, and more complete food landscape. The art of replacement is a key skill for a dietitian.

**Principle 3. "An Individual Diet is Always Possible"**

The proposed system is not dogma, but a living, adaptive tool. Any table and any standard protocol is merely a starting point. Age, gender, concomitant diagnoses, physical activity, cultural and taste preferences — all these are legitimate and mandatory variables to consider. Adaptation is not only possible but necessary. The only condition is that it must not violate the logic of the first two principles. Within their strict framework, a boundless field for personalization opens up, where diet becomes not a punishment, but a comfortable and conscious way of life.

These three principles will return again and again on the pages of this book.

## 1.3. The Role of Yeast: Why Not All Bread is the Same

One of the most unexpected prohibitions in the system is bread made with **thermophilic (brewer's) yeast**. In the modern world, this means practically all industrial bread: loaves, buns, factory-made lavash, puff pastry, most pizzas.

Thermophilic yeast (*Saccharomyces cerevisiae* in its industrial form) possess a property that makes them undesirable from the standpoint of intestinal physiology: they **survive at temperatures up to 45–50°C** and remain active even after baking at standard regimes. In the intestines, these yeasts continue fermentation, creating conditions for excessive gas formation, pH disturbance, and microbiota imbalance.

The permissible alternative is bread made with **wine yeast** (traditional sourdough, wild strain *Saccharomyces cerevisiae*) or bread on rye sourdough. Such bread undergoes long fermentation at low temperatures, and as a result of baking, the yeasts are completely inactivated.

In Georgian tradition, sourdough bread is the norm. *Tonis puri*, *mchadi* (cornbread — but corn is prohibited in the Tkemaladze system), *shoti* — all were historically prepared without industrial yeast. Returning to this tradition is not nostalgia, but a physiological choice.
## 1.4. Dairy Products: Brined Cheeses as an Exception

Milk and most dairy products are **prohibited** in the Tkemaladze-Gegeshidze system. This position causes the greatest surprise among people raised on Soviet dietetics, where kefir, cottage cheese, and milk were considered the foundation of healthy eating.

The rationale is simple: casein — the main milk protein — is a pro-inflammatory factor for most adults (especially after 40). Lactose creates a burden on the digestive system. Dairy products shift the intestinal pH to a state favorable for pathogenic microorganisms.

**The exception is brined cheeses** — Imeretian and *sulguni* (brined Georgian cheese). During the salting and fermentation process, casein is partially broken down, lactose goes into the brine, and the protein acquires a different structure — more accessible for digestion. This is not just a culinary tradition. It is a centuries-old milk processing technology that makes the product suitable for regular consumption.

Butter and *erba* (Georgian clarified butter) — are also permissible, as they contain minimal amounts of casein and lactose with a high concentration of saturated fats necessary for cell membranes.

---

## 1.5. Fruits Only in the First Half of the Day

A rule that may seem arbitrary at first glance has a clear physiological basis. Fruits contain simple sugars (fructose, glucose) and organic acids, which:

1.  **When consumed in the morning** — are quickly absorbed, provide energy, and do not compete with heavy food for enzymes.
2.  **When consumed in the evening** — enter the intestine, already filled with protein or starchy food. Their passage speed decreases. Fermentation begins. The result is bloating, sleep disturbance, and a burden on the liver at night.

Furthermore, **circadian rhythms of digestion** (established by chronobiology since the 1990s) show: the peak activity of digestive enzymes, insulin sensitivity, and bile secretion occurs in the first half of the day. Fruits eaten in the morning are processed optimally. In the evening, the same fruit creates a metabolic load.

The rule is simple: **fruits — until 2:00 PM, preferably — until 12:00 PM**. On an empty stomach or 2–3 hours after a light breakfast.

---

## 1.6. Water Before Meals: Ritual and Physiology

The Tkemaladze-Gegeshidze system prescribes drinking **at least 150 ml of water 5–30 minutes before each meal**. Not during the meal and not after — before.

The reason is that water drunk during a meal dilutes gastric juice and bile, reducing their concentration and, accordingly, their digestive effectiveness. Water drunk 10–20 minutes before a meal, on the contrary:
- Washes the stomach of previous contents
- Stimulates the production of gastric juice (reflexively)
- Reduces appetite, preventing overeating
- Prepares the mucosa for contact with food

This rule is universal and does not depend on the presence of diseases. It works equally for a healthy person and for a patient on dietary table No. 5 or No. 9.

---

## 1.7. Fasting as a Therapeutic Tool

In the Orthodox tradition, fasting on Wednesdays and Fridays is a religious practice. In the Tkemaladze-Gegeshidze system, it is considered simultaneously as a **physiological tool for detoxification and rest for the digestive system**.

Two fasting days per week (28% of the time) mean:
- Regular reduction of the load on the liver and gallbladder
- Reduced intake of animal proteins and fats
- Activation of autophagy (the process of cellular self-cleaning, described by Yoshinori Ohsumi, Nobel Prize 2016)
- Psychological switching: an attitude towards food as a conscious choice, not automatic consumption

The fasting table in this system is not starvation. It is fish, legumes (*lobio* — Georgian bean dish), nuts, grains, vegetables, and fermented foods. It is a complete diet, only temporarily excluding meat and dairy products.

For patients with chronic diseases (especially of the biliary system, pancreas, intestines), these two fasting days per week often provide a more stable therapeutic effect than strict adherence to a dietary table on the other five days.

# Chapter 2. Permitted Foods

*The system of Vasiko Tkemaladze and Lali Gegeshidze*

Permitted foods are not just a list of what "can be eaten." It is the architecture of a diet built on the principle of minimal load with maximum nutritional value. Each product on this list has been tested not by a laboratory, but by generations — and confirmed by the clinical practice of Dr. Jaba Tkemaladze.

---

## 2.1. Bread and Grains: The Foundation, But Not Any

Only **three grains** are permitted:

-   **Wheat** (grain, wheat porridge — *gomi* — Georgian wheat porridge) — the main cereal of the system. Contains gluten, but in traditional preparation (boiled porridge, sourdough bread) it is absorbed without burdening the intestines.
-   **Millet / *kotsi* (Georgian millet)** — the oldest cereal of the Caucasus. Does not contain gluten, rich in silicon and magnesium. Exceptionally valuable for elderly patients.
-   **Pearl barley / *tonis puri* (Georgian pearl barley)** — hulled barley. High β-glucan content lowers the glycemic index of the dish and supports intestinal microbiota.

**Bread is permitted only on wine yeast or sourdough.** Industrial bread on thermophilic yeast is prohibited (see Chapter 1.3).

---

## 2.2. Eggs: Time and Heat Treatment Matter

Eggs are permitted, but **the cooking method is strictly regulated**:

-   **Hard-boiled — at least 40 minutes from boiling.** This is a non-standard time (usually 10–12 minutes are recommended), and it is related to the principle of complete denaturation of egg proteins. With standard boiling, the protein does not coagulate completely, and part of avidin (an anti-nutritional factor that blocks vitamin B7) retains activity. 40 minutes guarantee complete inactivation.
-   **Fried over low heat on both sides until fully cooked** — the yolk must be completely dry, without a liquid center. Frying only in butter (*erba*) or refined sunflower oil.

**Prohibited** without exception: soft-boiled eggs, "in a bag," poached, sunny-side up (fried on one side only), raw eggs, eggs in liquid batter. Any method where the yolk remains liquid or the white is not fully heated is unacceptable.

---

## 2.3. Meat and Poultry: Exsanguination as a Principle

Meat of four types is allowed:
-   **Beef** — the main source of complete protein and iron.
-   **Skinless chicken** — light protein, but the skin contains concentrated fat with pro-inflammatory mediators.
-   **Lamb** — traditional meat of the Caucasus. When properly prepared — a valuable source of zinc and B vitamins.
-   **Skinless turkey** — the most dietary of the permitted meats.

**The principle of exsanguination and the first broth:**
The first broth when boiling any meat — **always discard**. This rule seems obvious in folk tradition but is often ignored in practice. The first broth contains:
-   Extractives (purines — provocateurs of gout)
-   Xenobiotics accumulated by the animal during its life (antibiotics, hormones, pesticides from feed)
-   Coagulated blood proteins (foam)

After discarding the first broth, the meat is covered with fresh water and boiling continues. The second broth is clean.

---

## 2.4. Fish: Freedom in Choice, Strictness in Preparation

Any fish is permitted — river and sea. This is one of the few categories where the Tkemaladze-Gegeshidze system does not impose species restrictions. The method of preparation is important:

-   **Fried with *tkemali* (Georgian plum sauce)** — the acidity of the plum neutralizes excess fat and enhances bile secretion.
-   **Boiled in *kinzmari* (Georgian cilantro sauce)** — a traditional Georgian recipe: fish, cilantro, garlic (boiled), vinegar or *tkemali*. Cilantro has chelating properties — binds heavy metals.
-   Baked, stewed.

Prohibited: fish in batter made with beer yeast, canned fish in tomato sauce (tomatoes are prohibited), fish products with added dairy components.
---

## 2.5. Allowed Dairy Products: Three Exceptions

Despite the general prohibition of dairy products (see Chapter 1.4), the following are permissible:

- **Imeretian cheese** — a young brined cheese with a neutral taste. During the salting process, most of the lactose is washed out. Rich in calcium and protein in an accessible form.
- ***Sulguni*** (brined, stretched cheese) — a pressed, stretched brined cheese. Similar properties, but with a denser texture and a pronounced fermented milk taste.
- **Butter / კარაქი** — in small quantities (on bread, in porridge). A source of saturated fats and fat-soluble vitamins A, D, E, K.
- ***Erbo*** (clarified butter / ghee) — butter purified of casein and water. A traditional product of Georgian and Indian cuisine (ghee). Tolerated even with lactose intolerance. High smoke point — optimal for frying.

---

## 2.6. Fats: Only Refined Sunflower Oil

Among vegetable oils, **only refined sunflower oil** is allowed. This point is perhaps the most counterintuitive for the modern reader raised on the cult of "healthy olive oil."

The system's explanation is as follows: **unrefined oils form aldehydes and peroxides during heat treatment** — oxidation products of polyunsaturated fatty acids, which are pro-inflammatory agents. Olive oil is no exception in this regard — its smoke point (160–190°C) is lower than frying temperature.

Refined sunflower oil has a smoke point of about 220°C and is chemically stable at culinary temperatures. For salads and cold dishes — clarified butter (*erbo*) or a small amount of butter.

---

## 2.7. Fermented and Pickled Products

This is one of the system's strongest points — and one of its most "Georgian" features:

- ***Jinjoli*** (pickled) / ჯონჯოლი — flowers of the prickly caper, fermented in brine. Rich in flavonoids, have a choleretic effect.
- **Pickled cucumber** / მჟავე კიტრი — unlike marinated (with vinegar), pickled cucumbers contain live lactobacilli. A pure probiotic.
- **Pickled garlic** / მჟავე ნიორი — fermentation reduces the irritating effect of allicin (raw garlic is prohibited), while preserving antibacterial properties.
- ***Tkemali*** without pepper and sugar, with boiled garlic / ტყემალი — a sauce made from sour plums. Choleretic, digestive, antioxidant action. A classic Georgian "probiotic" for meat and fish.

---

## 2.8. Nuts and Seeds

- **Walnut / ნიგოზი** — the main nut of the system. High content of ω-3 fatty acids, vitamin E, magnesium. Used in the basic sauce *bajhe* (nuts + water, no oil).
- ***Bajhe*** (nut sauce) with water — a traditional Georgian dressing for fish, vegetables, and beans. Water instead of oil is fundamentally important.
- **Pumpkin seeds / გოგრის თესლი** — a source of zinc, magnesium, iron. Especially recommended for men.

---

## 2.9. Legumes

- ***Lobio*** (beans) / ლობიო — the main protein product on fasting days. Rich in soluble fiber, lowers the glycemic index of the diet.
- **Green beans / მწვანე ლობიო** — unlike mature beans, contains less starch and more chlorophyll. An ideal side dish.

Important: peas, lentils, chickpeas — **prohibited** (despite also being legumes). The Tkemaladze-Gegeshidze system classifies them as products that cause excessive fermentation in the intestines.

# Chapter 3. Prohibited Products

*The System of Vasiko Tkemaladze and Lali Gegeshidze*

---

The list of prohibited products is not arbitrary or a "dietary fad." Behind each prohibition lies a specific physiological mechanism. Understanding the reasons is more important than simply following the rules: when a patient understands *why* something is harmful — they stop looking for "small exceptions."

But before reading this list — it is important to understand its philosophy. The word "prohibited" in the Tkemaladze-Gegeshidze system does not mean "deprivation" and does not mean "forever." It means: each product in this chapter has its own valuable component — protein, mineral, taste, cultural tradition. And for each of these values, there exists a cleaner source: one where the benefit exists, and the accompanying harm is absent or incomparably less.

Ask yourself about each prohibition: *"What exactly am I losing in this product — and where can I find the same thing without the problematic part?"* This chapter is not a wall. It is a map for finding better alternatives. The principle of replacement, not the principle of deprivation.

---
## 3.1. Bread with Thermophilic Yeast: The Silent Threat

This is prohibition number one — and the most important for understanding the logic of the entire system.

**Thermophilic yeast** (industrial strain *Saccharomyces cerevisiae*) was developed in the 1930s-40s to accelerate industrial bread production. Their main property is resistance to high temperatures. As a result:

1. During standard baking (180–200°C, 30–40 minutes) **some of the yeast survives** inside the crumb, where the temperature reaches only 95–98°C.
2. Upon entering the intestine, they continue fermentation, competing with the normal microbiota.
3. Their metabolic products — ethanol (in microdoses), CO₂, organic acids — alter the intestinal pH.

The prohibition includes: all industrial bakery products, store-bought loaves, most industrially produced lavash, fast-food restaurant pizza, puff pastry and yeast-based products.

**Not prohibited:** bread on natural sourdough (grape, rye, hop), *tonis puri* and shoti from traditional bakeries using sourdough, homemade bread from wheat flour without yeast.

---

## 3.2. Prohibited Grains: Explanation for Each

Five categories of cereals are prohibited:

### Semolina / მანის ბურღული
Wheat of a high degree of refinement, completely devoid of fiber, vitamins, and minerals. Practically pure starch with a high glycemic index. No nutritional value with significant glycemic load.

### Buckwheat / წიწიბური
A paradoxical prohibition for those familiar with buckwheat's "dietary" reputation. Modern nutrition science rightly evaluates buckwheat as a valuable source of rutin, magnesium, and slow carbohydrates. Nevertheless, in the Tkemaladze-Gegeshidze system, it is classified as prohibited. The basis — **empirical clinical observation**: in the practice of Dr. Jaba Tkemaladze, in a significant number of patients with biliary dyskinesia, dysbiosis, and irritable bowel syndrome, the exclusion of buckwheat consistently correlated with improvement. The presumed mechanism — individual hypersensitivity to buckwheat proteins and its influence on the microbiological profile — is not confirmed in randomized studies and requires investigation. Until evidence appears, this provision of the system should be considered a *traditional clinical observation*, not an established physiological fact. Replacement: millet (*gomi*), pearl barley, spelt.

### Peas, Lentils, Chickpeas / ბარდა, ოსპი, ყარყატი

## 3.10. The Balance Principle: Why a "Healthy" Product May Be Prohibited

A logical question arises: how can a system that restricts widely recommended products like olive oil, tomatoes, buckwheat, or kefir be justified? After all, their benefits are supported by research.

The key to understanding lies in **the first principle of the system: "You cannot do one thing while causing more harm."** The context of application decides everything. Yes, olive oil is a source of monounsaturated fats in the Mediterranean diet, and kefir contains probiotics. However, within the context of a specific clinical condition or constitutional type, as defined in the Tkemaladze-Gegeshidze system, the cumulative effect of a product may be negative. For example, if a product carries one proven benefit (potassium in potatoes, lycopene in tomatoes) but simultaneously creates three potential risks (glycemic load, irritating effect, disruption of enzymatic balance), this is not a compromise but a net loss for an organism requiring restoration.

This logic can be expressed by the formula: **if the ratio "benefit ÷ harm" < 1, the product requires replacement.** This is not dietary maximalism but applied health mathematics based on the principle of balance. The system, synthesized with Pevzner's approaches, acts as a precision filter: it does not deny the nutritional value of products "in a vacuum," but excludes those whose side effects in this specific context outweigh the benefits, creating a burden where there should be none.

Thus, the system does not propose dogmatic prohibitions but demonstrates that **there are no irreplaceable products — only an insufficiently studied list of alternatives**, devoid of specific drawbacks while offering similar nutritional value.

# **Chapter 4. Moderately Permitted Products: The Physiology of Limited Consumption**

Within the Nutrition System, developed based on a synthesis of the clinical protocols of Dr. Vasiko Tkemaladze and the dietetic principles of M.I. Pevzner, there exists a special category of products. They fall neither under the strict prohibition that disrupts homeostasis nor under the unconditional recommendation that supports physiological norm. These are products conditionally designated as **"moderately permitted."** Their consumption is permissible but strictly regulated in terms of volume, frequency, and, critically, the patient's health status. This category requires maximum clinical caution from the dietitian and discipline and precision from the patient in following prescriptions. The key principle: **"moderately" means "rarely, in small portions, and only in the absence of exacerbation of the primary or concomitant diseases."**

## **4.1. White and Brown Rice (ბრინჯი / *brinji*)**

**Physiological rationale for the "moderate" status:** Rice, especially polished white rice, is a source of easily digestible carbohydrates with a high glycemic index (GI). Its starch is quickly hydrolyzed into glucose, creating a significant load on the insulin-producing apparatus of the pancreas. Brown rice, which retains the bran layer, contains more fiber, magnesium, and B vitamins, somewhat slowing absorption. However, it can also concentrate phytic acid (an inhibitor of zinc and iron absorption) and, if stored improperly, mycotoxins.

**Recommendations:**
*   **Volume:** No more than 100–150 g of cooked product per serving.
*   **Frequency:** 1–2 times per week, no more.
*   **Preparation method:** Must be well-cooked, preferably as a thin porridge in water. The first water after boiling is recommended to be drained to remove some surface starch and possible impurities.
*   **Context:** Rice can be included in the diet as the most neutral grain product during recovery from acute gastroenterological diseases (e.g., after diarrhea), when mechanical and chemical sparing of the GI mucosa is necessary.

**Contraindications and limitations:**
*   **Type 2 diabetes mellitus, metabolic syndrome, insulin resistance:** Strictly limit or exclude. White rice is prohibited.
*   **Obesity:** Only brown rice is permissible in minimal quantities and not in the evening.
*   **Tendency to constipation (atonic):** White rice is excluded due to its constipating effect.
*   **Exacerbation of chronic pancreatitis, cholecystitis, gastritis:** Complete prohibition until stable remission is achieved.

**Georgian context:** In traditional cuisine, rice rarely serves as an independent side dish. Its moderate use in complex dishes, for example, as a filling for ***pkhali*** or a base for ***chvishtari*** (cornbread sometimes mixed with rice flour), historically aligns with the principle of limited consumption.

## **4.2. Cauliflower and Broccoli (ყვავილოვანი კომბოსტო / *k'vavilovani kombosto*, ბროკოლი / *brok'oli*)**

**Physiological rationale for the "moderate" status:** Unlike strictly prohibited white cabbage, these varieties contain less coarse fiber and sulfurous substances that cause flatulence and mucosal irritation. They are rich in vitamin C, K, folate, and sulforaphane (especially broccoli), which has proven antioxidant and potentially cancer-protective effects. However, they remain cruciferous vegetables containing complex oligosaccharides (raffinose) and purine bases that are difficult to digest.

**Recommendations:**
*   **Volume:** Portion no more than 150–200 g in cooked form.
*   **Frequency:** 2–3 times per week.
*   **Preparation method:** **Thermal processing only:** stewing, steaming, baking until completely soft. Raw consumption is categorically prohibited.
*   **Context:** Can serve as an alternative source of fiber and vitamins when it is necessary to diversify the vegetable diet.

**Contraindications and limitations:**
*   **Gout, hyperuricemia, urolithiasis (urate genesis):** Limit due to purine content.
*   **Hypothyroidism in the decompensation stage:** Excessive consumption of raw (which is prohibited in the system) or poorly cooked cruciferous vegetables can suppress thyroid function due to goitrogens, which are partially destroyed by heat treatment.
*   **Exacerbation of gastritis, pancreatitis, irritable bowel syndrome (IBS) with flatulence:** Exclude.

## **4.3. Pasta and Spaghetti from Wheat Flour (სპაგეტი / *spageti*)**

**Physiological rationale for the "moderate" status:** Quality pasta made from durum wheat has a lower GI compared to soft bread or porridge, as its starch is enclosed in a gluten protein network. It releases glucose more slowly. However, it is still a gluten-containing product that places a load on the intestinal mucosa and, in excess, can promote the growth of opportunistic flora.

**Recommendations:**
*   **Volume:** No more than 80–100 g of dry product (portion of cooked pasta ~250-300g).
*   **Frequency:** Once every 7–10 days.
*   **Preparation method:** Cooked **al dente** (to a slight firmness), with plenty of permitted herbs, stewed vegetables, or a lean meat sauce without tomatoes. Fatty cream and cheese sauces are prohibited.
*   **Context:** Permissible as an occasional carbohydrate variety for physically active individuals without signs of celiac disease.

**Contraindications and limitations:**
*   **Celiac disease, gluten intolerance, autoimmune bowel diseases:** Complete and lifelong prohibition.
*   **Diabetes mellitus, obesity:** Strictly limited.
*   **Intestinal dysbiosis, SIBO (small intestinal bacterial overgrowth):** Exclude until the condition is corrected.

## **4.4. Rabbit (კურდღელი / *kurdgheli*)**

**Physiological rationale for the "moderate" status:** Rabbit meat is considered dietary: it is lean, with a fine-fiber structure, rich in complete protein, iron, and vitamin B12. However, from the System's perspective, this is essentially the meat of a wild animal, even when farm-raised. Its protein structure and fatty acid composition may not be fully adapted for regular consumption by a person with weakened digestion. Like any meat, it requires thorough bleeding.

**Recommendations:**
*   **Volume:** Portion of 120–150 g in cooked form.
*   **Frequency:** No more than once a week.
*   **Preparation method:** Long stewing, steaming, or baking in foil. **The first broth after boiling must be discarded** to remove extractive substances, purines, and possible residues of medications used in animal husbandry.
*   **Context:** Can be used as an alternative protein source during periods when it is necessary to reduce the load of fatty acids (e.g., in hyperlipidemia) while maintaining the intake of heme iron.

**Contraindications and limitations:**
*   **Gout, urolithiasis:** Limit due to purines, despite their relatively low content.
*   **Severe kidney disease with impaired nitrogen excretion:** General protein restriction applies.
*   **Individual allergic reaction to this type of meat.**

## **4.5. Barberry / კოწახური (*kotsakhuri*) (Berberis vulgaris)**

**Physiological rationale for the "moderate" status:** Barberry berries have a pronounced choleretic, anti-inflammatory, and antiseptic effect due to their content of the alkaloid berberine. This is precisely the source of their potential benefit and simultaneous danger. Uncontrolled intake can lead to spasm of the bile ducts in the presence of stones, excessive stimulation of peristalsis, and irritation of mucous membranes.

**Recommendations:**
*   **Volume and form:** Only as a seasoning — dried and ground berries added to sauces (e.g., for fish) or meat dishes during stewing. No more than 1/2 teaspoon per dish.
*   **Frequency:** In dishes 1–2 times per week.
*   **Method of use:** **Prohibited in raw form and as concentrated decoctions/infusions** for self-treatment.

**Contraindications and limitations:**
*   **Cholelithiasis, biliary dyskinesia of the hyperkinetic type:** High risk of provoking colic.
*   **Pregnancy:** Berberine may stimulate uterine contractions.
*   **Hypotension:** May enhance the lowering of blood pressure.
*   **Acute inflammatory diseases of the GI tract (gastritis, enterocolitis).**
## **4.6. Strawberries (მარწყვი) and Almonds (ნუში)**

# **Chapter 5. Beverages and Infusions: Physiology, Chronomedicine, and Therapeutic Application**

**5.1. Water: Chronophysiology of Hydration and Digestion**

In the Tkemaladze–Gegeshidze system, water is not considered a neutral medium but an active physiological agent, the intake of which is strictly synchronized with the cycles of the gastrointestinal tract's secretory activity. The main principle: **intake of 150–200 ml of clean, non-carbonated water at room temperature 20–30 minutes before each main meal.**

*Physiological Rationale:*
1.  **Preparation of Mucous Membranes:** Water hydrates the mucous membranes of the mouth, esophagus, and stomach, preparing them for contact with the food bolus and ensuring optimal secretion of saliva and gastric juice.
2.  **Prevention of Concentrated Secretion:** Drinking water directly during or after a meal dilutes gastric juice (HCl and pepsin) and bile, disrupting the processes of protein denaturation and fat emulsification. This creates preconditions for fermentation and putrefaction processes in the lower sections of the intestine.
3.  **Activation of Motility:** A portion of water taken in advance gently stimulates peristalsis and signals the imminent arrival of food, triggering the stomach's "migrating motor complex."

*Therapeutic Effect in Chronic Diseases:*
-   **Gastritis, Duodenitis:** Proper water regimen prevents the aggressive effect of concentrated gastric juice on the inflamed mucosa.
-   **Biliary Dyskinesia:** Timely hydration promotes adequate thinning of bile and reduces the risk of its stagnation.
-   **Irritable Bowel Syndrome (IBS):** Organizes the rhythm of the gastrointestinal tract, reducing the likelihood of spasms and flatulence.

**5.2. Coffee: Secretory Chronotrigger**

The system categorically distinguishes between the effects of **instant coffee** and **brewed (sediment) coffee**. Only **instant coffee** is allowed, taken **strictly on an empty stomach in the morning or no earlier than 2–3 hours after a meal.**

*Physiological Rationale:*
1.  **Stimulation of Gastric Secretion:** Caffeine and chlorogenic acids are powerful stimulants of gastric parietal cells, increasing the secretion of hydrochloric acid. If coffee enters an empty stomach, the acid is used to prepare for food intake. If coffee is consumed during or immediately after a meal, excess acid leads to hypersecretion, which over time can cause gastritis, reflux, and impaired pyloric function.
2.  **The Sediment Problem:** Brewed coffee (Turkish, espresso) contains micro-particles of sediment and crema, which mechanically irritate and even scratch the mucosa of the gastric outlet and the sphincter of Oddi, which regulates the flow of bile and pancreatic juice. This is a direct path to dyskinesia, cholestasis, and pancreatitis.
3.  **Sugar:** Coffee with sugar, taken after a meal, creates a double carbohydrate load, provoking a sharp release of insulin and feeding pathogenic intestinal microflora.

*Therapeutic Effect:* Correct intake of instant coffee on an empty stomach can be beneficial for **hypoacid conditions** (low stomach acidity), gently stimulating secretion. However, in cases of peptic ulcer disease, hyperacid gastritis, and pronounced vegetative-vascular dystonia, its intake should be minimized.

**5.3. Medicinal Infusions: Postprandial Phytotherapy**

Infusions in the system are taken **1.5–2 hours after finishing a meal**, when the main food bolus has left the stomach. This is the "phytotherapeutic window."

*Preparation (Universal Method):* 1 teaspoon of dry plant material (leaves, berries, flowers) is poured with 200–250 ml of boiling water in a ceramic or glass container. The container is tightly closed and infused for **at least 8–12 hours (overnight)**. This method of cold infusion with boiling water allows for maximum extraction of biologically active substances (flavonoids, vitamins, essential oils in a gentle form) without destroying them with high heat and without extracting excessive tannins.

*Key Infusions and Their Actions:*

1.  **Savory (Kondari – *Satureja hortensis*):** "Sanitizer of the bronchopulmonary and genitourinary systems." Essential oils (carvacrol, thymol) have pronounced bactericidal and fungicidal effects. *Indicated for:* chronic bronchitis, cystitis, urethritis, as a mild anthelmintic.
2.  **Linden (Flowers):** "Capillary stabilizer." Flavonoids (rutin, quercetin) and phytosterols strengthen the vascular wall, reduce its fragility, and have a mild sedative and diaphoretic effect. *Indicated for:* vegetative-vascular dystonia, initial stages of hypertension, neuroses.
3.  **Blueberry/Blackberry (Leaf and Berry):** "Regulator of carbohydrate metabolism and visual pigment." Anthocyanins and myrtillin improve retinal trophism and increase cell sensitivity to insulin. *Indicated for:* insulin resistance, metabolic syndrome, increased visual load.
4.  **Saffron (Stigmas):** "Antioxidant and mood corrector." Crocin and safranal increase serotonin and dopamine levels without having a direct stimulating effect on the central nervous system. Has powerful anti-carcinogenic potential. *Indicated for:* depressive states, in the complex prevention of age-associated diseases.
5.  **"Detox" Blend (Beet Greens, Dill, Coriander Seeds):** Stimulates bile secretion and intestinal peristalsis, binds and removes metabolites. *Indicated for:* weekly course use for chronic constipation, sluggish liver.

**5.4. Mineral Waters: Acid-Base Balance as the Foundation**

The system makes a strict distinction between **acidic** and **alkaline** mineral waters, preferring the former.

*Physiological Rationale:* A healthy stomach has a sharply acidic environment (pH 1.5–3.0), necessary for pepsin activation, absorption of non-heme iron, and initial control of the microbial load of incoming food. Systematic intake of alkaline waters (pH >7) reduces the acid gradient of the gastrointestinal tract, disrupting the primary breakdown of protein and reducing the effectiveness of digestive enzymes at the initial stage. Acidic waters, on the contrary, support the natural pH gradient, promoting optimal pepsin function and mineral absorption.

*Therapeutic Waters of Georgia:*
-   **"Nabeglavi" (acidic, pH ~4.5):** Rich in free carbon dioxide and magnesium ions. *Indications:* hypoacid gastritis, intestinal atony, conditions after food poisoning. Taken 15–20 minutes before meals, 100–150 ml.
-   **"Likani" (slightly alkaline, but of unique composition):** Contains a unique complex of silicic acid and organic iron. *Indications:* anemia, recovery after blood loss, diseases of the musculoskeletal system. In the Tkemaladze system, it is used with caution, **only outside of meals** (2–3 hours after), as a therapeutic course, not a daily drink.

**5.5. Green Tea: Its Place in the System**

Green tea, despite the widespread belief in its benefits, **is not a recommended beverage** in the classical system. Its high content of tannins (tannic substances) interferes with the absorption of non-heme iron from plant foods and can provoke constipation. Its tonic effect often precedes the gentle, harmonizing action of the permitted infusions. In exceptional cases, a weak infusion of green tea may be allowed in the first half of the day, but not as a systematic practice.

**Prohibited Beverages (Summary of Chapter 3):**
-   **Carbonated Waters and Lemonades:** Free carbon dioxide stretches the stomach walls, provokes reflux, and sugar causes dysbiosis.
-   **Packaged Juices and Nectars:** Concentrate of free sugars without fiber.
-   **Alcohol (except for dry wine in quantities of 50-100 ml, 2-3 times a week, separately from meals):** Ethyl alcohol is a hepatotoxin and mutagen.
-   **Tea Bags:** Typically contain low-quality raw materials with high tannin content and traces of chemical reagents from paper processing.

**Chapter Conclusion:** Beverages in the Tkemaladze–Gegeshidze system are not a means of quenching thirst, but **pharmacologically precise tools for managing secretion, motility, and the microbiome**. Their intake is strictly tied to circadian and digestive rhythms. Water prepares, coffee stimulates on an empty stomach, infusions treat in the interdigestive period, and mineral waters correct pH. Violating these rules negates efforts to build a proper diet from permitted foods.

# **Chapter 6. Age-Related and Physiological Adaptations of the Nutritional System**

## **6.1. Nutritional Strategy After 45–50 Years: From Raw to Processed**

Upon reaching the age of 45–50, the body's metabolic plasticity significantly decreases. This dictates the need for a strategic revision of the dietary approaches laid out in the Tkemaladze–Gegeshidze system. The key principle: **minimizing raw fruits and vegetables in favor of thermally processed ones.**

**Physiological Rationale:**
1.  **Reduced Exocrine Function of the Pancreas:** Age-related decrease in the secretion of amylase, lipase, and proteases by 30–40% complicates the primary hydrolysis of the complex structures of raw plant fiber and proteins.
2.  **Changes in Gastrointestinal Motility and Mucosal State:** Atrophic changes in the intestinal mucosa, decreased smooth muscle tone, and weakened peristalsis make coarse raw fiber (e.g., from white cabbage, raw carrots, leaf lettuce) not only useless but also traumatic. It can provoke flatulence, spasms, and mechanical irritation, exacerbating "leaky gut" syndrome.
3.  **Impaired Bile Flow:** With age, bile viscosity increases and the contractile function of the gallbladder decreases. Cold and hard-to-digest raw foods (especially in combination with prohibited olive oil or nuts) increase the risk of cholestasis.

**Practical Adaptations:**
*   **Vegetables:** Complete transition to **stewed, steamed, baked, or boiled** vegetables. Thermal processing partially destroys cellulose walls, making nutrients (carotenoids from carrots, lycopene from tomatoes after brief processing) more bioavailable and the product itself more easily digestible. Permitted vegetables (zucchini, cauliflower, broccoli, green beans, beets) are cooked until soft.
*   **Fruits:** Permitted fruits (apples, pears, plums) **only in the first half of the day (before 1:00 PM), only in the form of purees, baked, or in unsweetened compotes.** Portion size is reduced to 100–150 g. This allows for obtaining vitamins and antioxidants without overloading enzyme systems or provoking fermentation in the evening when digestive activity naturally decreases.
*   **Proteins:** Emphasis on **easily digestible proteins** — hard-boiled eggs (yolk should be completely dry), tender fish, pasture-raised poultry. Meat broths, even "second" ones, are used with caution, based on good tolerance.

## **6.2. Sleep and Work Regimen as an Integral Component of the Nutritional Profile**

Nutrition does not exist outside of circadian rhythms. The Tkemaladze–Gegeshidze system integrates chronobiology as a mandatory element.

**Circadian Rhythms of Digestion:**
*   **Breakfast (before 9:00 AM):** Peak secretion of cortisol and gastric juice. Ideal time for intake of complete protein (eggs, cheese) and complex carbohydrates (permitted cereals) to kickstart metabolism.
*   **Lunch (1:00–2:00 PM):** Maximum activity of digestive enzymes. The main meal, including animal protein, fats, and vegetables.
*   **Dinner (before 7:00 PM):** Should be completed at least 3 hours before sleep. Consists of light proteins (fish, poultry) and thermally processed vegetables. **Fruits and simple carbohydrates are categorically excluded.**

**"Fasting Window" and Autophagy:**
A 12–14-hour overnight break in eating (e.g., from 7:00 PM to 7–9:00 AM) is not just rest for the gastrointestinal tract, but a powerful **geroprotective tool**. Cellular nutrient deficiency activates the processes of autophagy — the utilization of damaged cellular components and pathogenic protein aggregates. This is a fundamental mechanism for preventing neurodegenerative diseases, oncology, and systemic inflammation.

**Risk of Undernutrition in the Elderly:**
Decreased sense of smell, taste, and appetite with age does not negate the need for quality nutrition. Strategy:
1.  **Increasing Nutritional Density:** Adding permitted refined oils, butter, egg yolks to prepared dishes to increase caloric content without increasing volume.
2.  **Culinary Processing:** Preparing soufflés, pâtés, cream soups based on permitted vegetables and meat to facilitate chewing and absorption.
3.  **Strict Control of Protein Intake:** At least 1–1.2 g per kg of body weight to prevent sarcopenia.

## **6.3. Children's Nutrition (3–15 years): Forming a Healthy Microbiome**

The child's body is a growing and plastic system, but the foundation of gastrointestinal health is laid precisely during this period. Adaptation of the system is based on **gradual tightening**.

**What is additionally permitted (compared to the adult protocol):**
*   **Dairy Products:** Up to 7–8 years old, **whole milk, cottage cheese, natural yogurt** are allowed in the first half of the day. This is a source of calcium for bone growth and casein, which in childhood, in the absence of intolerance, does not cause the same autoimmune reaction as in adults. By 12–15 years, a smooth transition to the adult protocol occurs (emphasis on butter and aged cheeses).
*   **Fruits:** Wider variety and consumption time. Permitted fruits can be given not only before lunch but also for an afternoon snack. However, **grapes, bananas, and citrus fruits are strictly excluded** (as highly allergenic and fermentation-provoking).
*   **Carbohydrates:** "Children's" cereals (millet, semolina), whole-grain bread on homemade sourdough are included more often.
**What is followed strictly:**
1.  **Ban on commercial yeast:** All industrial bread and pastries are excluded. This is the primary measure for preventing childhood dysbiosis, allergies, and zinc deficiency.

# Chapter 7. History and Methodology of Dietary Tables

## 7.1. Manuil Isaakovich Pevzner (1872–1952) — Biography and Scientific Path

Manuil Isaakovich Pevzner, born in 1872, is a key figure in the history of domestic clinical dietetics. After receiving his medical education at Moscow University, he trained under the guidance of the outstanding therapist A.A. Ostroumov, which shaped his clinical thinking focused on a holistic approach to the patient. Working in the clinic, Pevzner noted the systematic lack of scientifically based nutritional recommendations for patients with various nosologies. This observation formed the basis of his further scientific work.

In 1930, Pevzner became one of the founders and the first director of the State Central Institute of Nutrition of the People's Commissariat of Health of the RSFSR in Moscow. This institute became a platform for large-scale research and organizational work. It was here that Pevzner formulated and implemented the revolutionary-for-its-time concept of "therapeutic nutrition" as an independent, full-fledged medical discipline, not an auxiliary or empirical practice. He argued that diet therapy should be strictly differentiated, pathogenetically justified, and occupy an equal position with pharmacotherapy in complex treatment.

The main work of his life was the development of the system of "therapeutic tables" — standardized diets, each intended for a specific group of diseases. After Pevzner's death in 1952, his system was officially codified and approved by the Order of the USSR Ministry of Health, becoming a mandatory standard for all medical and preventive institutions of the country for decades to come.

## 7.2. Structure of the System: 15 Main Tables and Variants (a, b, c, d)

The Pevzner system is a hierarchical structure of 15 main diets (tables), numbered not randomly but in accordance with clinical and physiological logic. Numbers 1–4 traditionally refer to diets with maximum sparing of the gastrointestinal tract (GIT), used in acute conditions or exacerbations of chronic diseases (peptic ulcer disease, gastritis, enterocolitis). Tables 5, 6, 7, 8, 9, 10 focus on correcting metabolic disorders and functions of specific organs: liver and biliary tract (5), purine metabolism (6), kidneys (7), energy balance (8), carbohydrate metabolism (9), cardiovascular system (10). Tables 11, 13, 14, 15 are of a general strengthening or specialized nature (tuberculosis, acute infections, oxalaturia, general diet).

The most important methodological tool of the system became sub-variants of the main tables, denoted by letters (1a, 1b, 5p, etc.). They represent stages of therapeutic nutrition, allowing a smooth transition of the patient from a maximally gentle diet in the acute period to a more challenging one in the remission phase. For example, table 1a (maximally mechanically and chemically gentle) is replaced by table 1b (less strict), and then by the main table 1. This gradation ensured continuity and consistency of diet therapy.

In the context of Soviet medicine, the Pevzner system became a model of standardization. It made it possible to unify hospital nutrition across the vast country, providing a basic, reproducible, and controlled therapeutic diet for millions of patients. Dietitians and doctors worked according to unified tables and technological charts, which undoubtedly increased process manageability, although it limited individual approach.

## 7.3. Principles of Diet Prescription

Pevzner's methodology is based on several fundamental physiological principles that remain relevant in dietetics.

1.  **The principle of sparing:** Most clearly represented in tables 1 and 4. It includes:
    *   **Mechanical sparing:** Exclusion of coarse fiber, use of pureed, chopped, mashed dishes. In the Tkemaladze-Gegeshidze system, this principle transforms into mandatory thorough chewing (21–40 times) and preference for well-cooked, easily digestible foods, for example, long-stewed *lobio* (beans) or well-boiled eggs.
    *   **Chemical sparing:** Exclusion of substances that stimulate secretion or irritate the mucous membrane (extractive substances from broths, organic acids, essential oils). A key divergence arises here: Pevzner widely used milk for chemical sparing, whereas the Tkemaladze protocol considers milk casein as a powerful chemical irritant and provocateur of an immune response.
    *   **Thermal sparing:** Serving food warm, excluding very hot and cold dishes. Both systems agree on this point.

2.  **The principle of restriction/exclusion of certain nutrients or substances:** This is the basis of metabolic tables.
    *   Protein restriction in chronic renal failure (table 7).
    *   Purine restriction in gout (table 6).
    *   Restriction of easily digestible carbohydrates and fats in diabetes and obesity (tables 9, 8).
    *   Sodium restriction in hypertension (table 10).

3.  **The principle of function stimulation:** Applied in cases of insufficiency of secretory or motor function. A classic example is table 2 for hypoacid gastritis, which includes moderate chemical stimulants (weak broths, juices) and dishes requiring active digestion. In the Georgian tradition, the role of mild digestive stimulants is performed by spices and fermented foods: *kondari* (savory), *tkemali* (plum sauce) or *jinjoli* (fermented acacia shoots).

4.  **Temporary vs permanent diets:** Pevzner clearly distinguished diets for the acute period (temporary, strict, e.g., 1a, 4) and diets for long-term, often lifelong adherence in the remission phase or for chronic incurable conditions (tables 5, 7, 9, 10). This distinction remains relevant.

## 7.4. Limitations of the Classical System: Products Contradicting the Tkemaladze System

Despite its historical significance and clinical logic, the Pevzner system, created in the 1930s–1950s, reflects the level of scientific knowledge of its era. From the standpoint of modern data on the microbiome, chronic systemic inflammation, food immunology, and molecular dietetics, significant limitations are found in it, which directly conflict with Dr. Tkemaladze's protocol.

1.  **Milk and dairy products.** Are the cornerstone of many Pevzner tables (1, 2, 5, 6, 7, 9, 10, 11). Milk was considered an ideal product for sparing, a source of protein and calcium. However, from the perspective of the Tkemaladze protocol, milk casein (especially cow's) is a foreign protein that provokes antibody production and triggers an autoimmune cascade. Lactose creates a load on the enzymatic systems of an adult. Kefir, yogurt, cottage cheese, and sour cream allowed by Pevzner are strictly prohibited in the Tkemaladze system. Alternative sources of calcium in the Georgian system are cucumbers, green beans, brine-aged cheeses (*sulguni*), where casein is partially decomposed.

2.  **Buckwheat.** Recommended in tables 5 and 9 as a useful, hypoglycemic grain. The Tkemaladze protocol categorically prohibits buckwheat, considering it a product that promotes the growth of fungal microflora in the intestines and has high allergenic potential. Instead, wheat, millet, and pearl barley are offered.

3.  **Potatoes.** Widely allowed in most tables as a source of starch. In the Tkemaladze protocol, potatoes are classified as one of the five most harmful foods for modern humans due to their high glycemic index and oxalate content.

4.  **Tomatoes.** Permissible in tables 5, 7, 9, 10. According to the Tkemaladze protocol, alpha-tomatine (a saponin) in tomatoes irritates the bile duct, disrupting bile flow and promoting cholestasis. In Georgian cuisine, tomatoes are often subjected to prolonged heat treatment in sauces (for example, in *bajhe*), which may partially reduce their irritating potential, but they are not recommended raw.

5.  **Olive oil.** Recommended in tables 5, 7, 10 as a source of healthy fats. The Tkemaladze protocol prohibits all unrefined oils, including olive oil, as they can irritate the bile duct and oxidize when heated. The permitted fat is refined sunflower oil, as well as animal fats — butter and ghee.

6.  **Honey.** Allowed in a number of tables as a natural sweetener. In the Tkemaladze system, honey is prohibited due to its free fructose content, which creates a direct toxic load on the liver, contributing to the development of non-alcoholic fatty liver disease.

**Conclusion:** The system of therapeutic tables by M.I. Pevzner is a grand monument to clinical thought of the first half of the 20th century, which laid the foundations for a systematic approach to diet therapy. Its strength lies in its clear structure, standardization, and pathogenetic logic. However, its weakness stems from the historical context of its creation: lack of knowledge about the gut microbiome, the role of chronic low-grade inflammation (inflammaging), molecular mechanisms of food intolerance, and the influence of food exosomes on gene expression. The protocol of Dr. Tkemaladze, synthesizing modern scientific data with the principles of traditional Georgian gastronomy, offers a radically updated paradigm in which many of Pevzner's "gold standards" are reconsidered from the standpoint of gut health, immune tolerance, and metabolic longevity.

## 10.3. Proteins: Meat and Fish — Preparation Principles

The synthesis in this category concerns not so much the list of products as the technologies for their processing.

*   **Beef, Lamb, Poultry**. Both systems agree on recommending lean cuts. **Key synthesis rule**: mandatory bleeding and discarding the first broth. Physiological rationale (Tkemaladze): removal of extractive substances, stress hormones, and xenobiotics accumulated in animal tissues. This directly correlates with Pevzner's principles of sparing (tables 1, 5, 7), where secondary broths are recommended. Meat must be thoroughly cooked. Poultry skin is removed.

This Tkemaladze requirement has a physiological basis: prolonged thermal processing ensures complete denaturation of protein inhibitors (ovomucoid, avidin), which can irritate the GI mucosa and bind biotin. *   **Legumes: The case of *lobio* (*ლობიო*)**. A vivid example of conflict.

For healthy individuals and with other pathologies (obesity, diabetes, constipation), *lobio* is an excellent dish. Requires thorough preparation with pre-soaking and prolonged cooking to reduce oligosaccharides.

## 10.4. Fats: Only Permitted Oils

*   **Butter**. Unanimously permitted in moderate amounts (Pevzner — tables 1, 5, 11; Tkemaladze — recommended).

*   ***Erba* (ghee) (*ერბო*)**. Not mentioned by Pevzner, but is the **optimal choice in synthesis**. Physiological rationale: during the clarification process, water, milk sugar, and proteins (casein) are removed, leaving pure milk fat with a high smoke point. This makes ghee ideal for frying and stewing, aligning with the principles of sparing (does not form carcinogens) and safe from the perspective of casein allergy.

*   **Refined Sunflower Oil**. Permitted by Tkemaladze, neutral by Pevzner. In synthesis — **the main vegetable oil for frying and dressing**. Its neutrality and stability when heated are advantages.

*   **Olive Oil**. Recommended in Pevzner's tables (5, 7, 10) as a source of healthy fats. **Strictly prohibited** by Tkemaladze (irritates the bile duct, especially unrefined). **Synthesis**: **Prohibited**. This conflict is resolved in favor of prohibition due to the potential risk to the biliary system. Beneficial monounsaturated fats in the synthesized system are suggested to be obtained from avocado, olives (not oil), and nuts.

*   **Exception: Brined cheeses aged 40+ days**. These include *sulguni* and *Imeretian cheese* — both of Georgian type, where during the brining process casein is partially broken down and lactose leaches into the brine. Bryndza (a brined cheese of non-Georgian type) is not included in the list of permitted items in the system, as its fermentation level can vary technologically. Preference is given to *sulguni* after 40 days of aging.

# Chapter 11. Fasts and Vegetarian Variants of Dietary Tables

## 11.1. Orthodox Fasting in a Clinical Context

Orthodox fasting is not merely a religious tradition but a complex, cyclical system of eating behavior with a pronounced physiological impact. Its structure includes four multi-day periods: Great Lent (40 days before Easter), Apostles' Fast (from Pentecost to the Feast of Saints Peter and Paul), Dormition Fast (14 days before the Dormition), and Nativity Fast (40 days before Christmas), as well as weekly fast days — Wednesday and Friday. In Georgian Orthodoxy, where fasting is observed with particular strictness, this practice has historically formed an extensive layer of national cuisine based on plant and fish products. Dishes such as *pkhali* from various herbs and vegetables, *lobiani* (bean-filled pie), or *ajapsandali* adapted to the Tkemaladze system (without eggplants and tomatoes) are inherently fasting foods.

From a clinical perspective, fasting creates conditions for periodic reduction of protein, particularly animal, load on the digestive and excretory systems. This contributes to lowering the level of insulin-like growth factor 1 (IGF-1), which, according to modern research, is one of the triggers for activating autophagy — the intracellular process of recycling damaged organelles and proteins. Periods of caloric restriction and changes in macronutrient composition also stimulate detoxification pathways, particularly the cytochrome P450 system in the liver, and may improve insulin sensitivity.

However, fasting has strict clinical contraindications. It is categorically not applicable for Table No. 11 (tuberculosis, exhaustion, postoperative period), during acute infectious diseases (Table No. 13), during periods of active growth in children, and in a number of endocrine and metabolic disorders in the decompensation stage.

Dr. Jaba Tkemaladze uses the principles of fasting as a therapeutic tool, adapting them to the strict framework of his protocol. In his practice, fasting is not a rejection of animal protein in favor of potatoes and tomatoes (prohibited products), but a transition to a balanced plant-fish diet with an emphasis on permitted vegetables, legumes, nuts, and fermented foods. This approach allows achieving the positive physiological effects of fasting while avoiding food triggers that could harm patients with pathologies of the gastrointestinal tract, biliary tract, and microbiome.

## 11.2. The Fasting Basic Table of the Tkemaladze System

Fasting nutrition in the Tkemaladze system is built on a clear division of days: strict (without any animal food) and non-strict (with permission for fish and, in certain periods, eggs). The key task is to ensure a complete protein and nutrient composition while excluding all prohibited products, even those traditionally considered "fasting."

**Permissible Protein Sources:**
*   **Fish:** On non-strict fasting days, all fish is permitted, especially fatty varieties (mackerel, sardines, salmon) as a source of omega-3 PUFAs.
*   **Eggs:** Permitted only on those fasting days allowed by church rules, and exclusively hard-boiled (cooked for 40–90 minutes) or well-fried.
*   **Legumes:** *Lobio (ლობიო)* from red or white beans, as well as green string beans, become the main plant protein. Thorough cooking is important for improved digestibility.
*   **Nuts:** Walnuts (only freshly cracked) and almonds serve as a protein "anchor." *Bajhe* — a walnut sauce with water — is a classic Georgian fasting dish, providing not only protein but also healthy fats.

**Carbohydrates and Fiber:** Porridges made from wheat, millet, pearl barley, and moderately rice are permitted. Bread — only on homemade wine yeast.

**Fats:** During strict fasting, exclusively refined sunflower oil is used. Butter and ghee are excluded.

**Sample Menu for a Strict Fasting Day According to the Tkemaladze System:**
*   **Breakfast:** Wheat porridge on water with the addition of refined sunflower oil. Apple (only before lunch).
*   **Second Meal:** A handful of freshly cracked walnuts.
*   **Lunch:** Pumpkin and zucchini cream soup. Stewed *lobio (ლობიო)* with onion, cilantro, and *utskho suneli*. Pickled cucumbers or *jinjoli (ჯონჯოლი)*.
*   **Dinner:** *Pkhali* from boiled beets with walnuts, cilantro, and spices. Bread on wine yeast.

**Categorically Prohibited During Fasting,** even according to traditional canons, but per Tkemaladze's protocol: tomatoes (irritate the bile duct), eggplants (oxalates), potatoes (harmful product), and buckwheat (fungal growth in the intestines).

### **13.1. Principles of Thermal Processing: Biochemical Transformation as the Basis of Therapy**

In the Tkemaladze-Gegeshidze system, thermal processing is a tool for managing nutrient bioavailability and neutralizing potentially harmful compounds. The principle is based on the understanding that many plant products contain enzyme inhibitors, lectins, and complex protein structures that can provoke an immune response and disrupt gastrointestinal function in patients with increased intestinal permeability syndrome, allergies, and chronic inflammatory conditions.

*   **Long-term stewing and boiling** (vegetables, legumes, meat) promotes the hydrolysis of complex polysaccharides and denaturation of proteins, making them more accessible for digestion and reducing the antigenic load.
*   **Frying after boiling** (e.g., for meat) is used to form a safe crust, excluding the consumption of undercooked product.
*   **Prohibition on frying in large amounts of oil** is associated with the risk of carcinogen and trans-fat formation, which contradicts the anti-inflammatory focus of the system.

**Clinical Example:** Patient K., 45 years old, diagnosed with "nonspecific ulcerative colitis in remission." Upon transitioning to the system using long-term thermal processing of legumes (*lobio*) and mandatory discarding of the first meat broth, he noted a reduction in flatulence, heaviness in the right hypochondrium, and normalization of stool within 3 weeks. This is associated with a reduced load of lectins and extractive substances on the inflamed mucosa.

### **13.2. Proper Egg Preparation: Long Boiling Protocol (40–90 minutes)**

**Clinical Rationale:** Egg white contains a number of proteins (ovalbumin, ovomucoid, lysozyme) that are strong allergens. Long-term thermal processing leads to deep denaturation of these proteins, changing their spatial configuration and significantly reducing their allergenic potential. Furthermore, it is assumed that this method completely neutralizes possible pathogens (e.g., salmonella) not only on the surface but also deep within the yolk, which remains less heated during standard boiling.

**Recipe: Eggs Boiled According to the Tkemaladze-Gegeshidze System**

*   **Ingredients:** Chicken or quail eggs (preferably from free-range chickens), water, a pinch of sea salt.
*   **Preparation:**
    1.  Place eggs at room temperature carefully into a pot with cold water so that the water covers them by 2-3 cm.
    2.  Add a pinch of salt (to prevent shell cracking).
    3.  Place on high heat, bring to a boil.
    4.  Reduce heat to minimum so the water barely simmers.
    5.  Boil **from 40 minutes (for quail) to 90 minutes (for large chicken eggs)**. It is permissible to boil longer — up to 2 hours. The egg will turn into a dense, homogeneous mass with a uniformly cooked yolk of a sandy color.
    6.  Pour cold water over the cooked eggs, peel. Use in salads, for preparing *pkhali* (vegetable pâté), or as an independent protein component for breakfast (strictly before noon).

**Variation "Fried Egg According to the System":** Fry in butter (ghee) under a lid on low heat for at least 10-12 minutes, until the white becomes completely matte and dense, and the yolk is fully set.

### **13.3. Bread on Homemade/Wine Yeast: Recipe and Rationale**

**Clinical Rationale:** The prohibition on thermophilic yeast (*Saccharomyces cerevisiae*) used in industrial baking is related to their high temperature resistance. According to the authors of the system, they are capable of surviving at body temperature, colonizing the intestine, disrupting symbiosis with the native microflora, and consuming B vitamins. Homemade ("wild") or wine yeast have a different species affiliation and structure, are more easily digested, and do not possess such heat resistance, performing only the function of a leavening agent for dough without negative consequences for the microbiome.

**Recipe: Wheat-Rye Bread on Grape Starter**

*   **Ingredients for the starter:** 100 g of mashed clean dark grapes (with a white bloom — natural yeast), 100 g of whole-grain rye flour, 100 ml of warm water (not above 35°C).
*   **Ingredients for the dough:** 500 g of flour mixture (whole-grain wheat + rye), 300 ml of water, 10 g of sea salt, all of the ready starter.
*   **Starter preparation (5-7 days):** Mix the mashed grapes with flour and water in a glass jar, cover with gauze. Keep at 22-25°C, "feeding" daily with 50 g of flour and 50 ml of water, removing part of the old mass. The ready starter actively bubbles and has a sourish aroma.
*   **Bread preparation:**
    1.  Mix all the starter, water, and flour in a bowl. Leave for 30-60 minutes (autolysis).
    2.  Add salt, knead into an elastic dough.
    3.  Fermentation at room temperature for 6-8 hours with 2-3 folds.
    4.  Shape a loaf, let it proof for 2-3 hours.
    5.  Bake in a preheated oven at 240°C with steam for the first 15 minutes, then reduce to 200°C and bake for another 40-50 minutes until done. Cool on a rack.

### **13.8. Proper Broths: The First Broth is Discarded — Why and How**

**Clinical rationale:** The primary broth, especially when using meat from industrial farming, concentrates extractive substances (purine bases, creatine, creatinine), residues of hormonal drugs, antibiotics, and products of cellular metabolism. These components create an excessive load on the liver and kidneys and can provoke allergic and pseudo-allergic reactions. Discarding the first broth is an effective way to remove them.

**Broth preparation protocol:**
1.  Thoroughly rinse the meat (beef, poultry), if necessary, soak for 1-2 hours in cold water.
2.  Cover with cold water, bring to a rapid boil.
3.  **Boil on high heat for 5-10 minutes, then discard all the water.**
4.  Rinse the meat under running water, clean the pot.
5.  Cover the meat with new cold water, bring to a boil, skim off the foam.
6.  Simmer on very low heat (without vigorous boiling) for 2-3 hours for poultry, 3-4 hours for red meat. The finished broth should be clear.

### **13.9. Desserts Made from Permitted Fruits (Only for the First Half of the Day)**

# **CHAPTER 14. INTEGRATION INTO LIFE: FROM PROTOCOL TO LIFESTYLE**

Implementing any therapeutic nutrition system faces the main challenge: how to turn a set of rules into an organic, sustainable part of a person's daily existence. The synthesis of the Tkemaladze-Gegeshidze and Pevzner systems offers not just a diet, but a holistic philosophy of interacting with food, based on a deep understanding of physiological rhythms and food biochemistry. ## **14.1. Daily Routine: Chronobiology of Nutrition**

The key integration principle is respect for the body's circadian rhythms. The system prescribes clear temporal structuring of meals, which aligns with modern concepts of chronobiology.

**Morning (6:00 – 12:00): Cleansing and light energy phase.**
*   **On an empty stomach:** A glass of warm structured water 20-30 minutes before the first meal. This initiates peristalsis and prepares the GI tract.
*   **Breakfast (7:00 – 9:00):** Emphasis on low-glycemic carbohydrates and fruits. Porridges (except buckwheat) with water or with added butter, bread on homemade yeast are acceptable. **Fruits are consumed exclusively during this period.** This provides the body with quick energy and vitamins, which will be fully absorbed during the active phase of the day.
*   **Example:** Spelt porridge with a baked apple and a spoonful of butter; bread with *sulguni*; herbal tea.

**Day (12:00 – 17:00): Activity and main nutrition phase.**
*   **Lunch (13:00 – 15:00):** The main meal. This is the time of peak digestive enzyme activity. A protein component (bled meat or fish) must be included, combined with thermally processed vegetables (except nightshades). Soups on bone broth.
*   **Example:** Broth from young lamb; boiled beef with stewed carrots and zucchini; salad of boiled beets with prunes.

**Evening (after 17:00): Rest and minimal food load phase.**
*   **Dinner (no later than 19:00):** Light, predominantly protein or vegetable. Brined cheeses (*sulguni*, Imeretian), boiled eggs (cooked for 40-90 minutes), stewed vegetables are ideal. Complete exclusion of raw vegetables, fruits, and heavy carbohydrates.
*   **Example:** Baked fish with rosemary; hard-boiled egg (40+ min) with herbs; *sulguni* with cucumber (peeled).

## **14.2. Physical Activity and Nutrition: Synchronization**

Nutrition must be synchronized with the physical activity regimen for optimal recovery and to avoid metabolic stress.
*   **Before training (1.5-2 hours before):** A light meal rich in complex carbohydrates (porridge, bread) is recommended. This will provide energy without a feeling of heaviness.
*   **After training (within 60-90 minutes):** It is necessary to replenish protein for muscle repair. A portion of boiled meat or brined cheese with stewed vegetables is ideal. **It is critically important** to follow the drinking regimen: drink water 20-30 minutes *before* this meal.
*   **On days of high activity:** Caloric intake should be increased by adding portions of butter and permitted grains at lunch, not by increasing the evening meal.

## **14.3. Seasonal Adaptation of the Diet**

The system lives in harmony with natural cycles, which is a legacy of both Georgian and Soviet dietetics schools.
*   **Spring:** Emphasis on greens (nettle, wild garlic, cilantro), light vegetable soups, lean broths for gentle detox. Reduce the proportion of red meat.
*   **Summer:** Abundance of local non-starchy vegetables (zucchini, cucumbers, green beans), herbs, berries (before noon). Meat — predominantly poultry and young lamb. Increase water intake.
*   **Autumn:** Introduction of denser, warming dishes. Emphasis on root vegetables (carrots, beets, turnips), pumpkin, seasonal fruits (apples, pears — in the morning). Increase the proportion of animal fats (butter).
*   **Winter:** Predominance of warm, energy-dense foods. Rich bone broths, stewed meat, baked vegetables. Brined cheeses and butter as a source of fat-soluble vitamins and energy.

## **14.4. Transition Period: How to Switch from a Habitual Diet to the System**

A sharp transition is stressful for the psyche and microbiome. A phased integration over 4-6 weeks is recommended.

**Weeks 1-2: Cleansing and preparation phase.**
*   The most problematic products are excluded: **tomatoes, eggplants, potatoes, pork, raw milk**.
*   Begin practicing **drinking water 20-30 minutes before meals**.
*   Gradually shift fruits to the first half of the day.
*   Introduce **bread on homemade yeast**, replacing regular bread.

**Weeks 3-4: Structural restructuring phase.**
*   Complete refusal of **buckwheat and thermophilic yeast**.
*   Implementation of rules for preparing **eggs (40-90 minutes of boiling)** and **bleeding meat**.
*   Clear separation of meals by time (breakfast, lunch, light dinner).
*   Introduction of **brined cheeses and butter** into the diet.

**Weeks 5-6: Consolidation and fine-tuning phase.**
*   Full compliance with all time and product restrictions.
*   Adaptation of the diet to the season.
*   Observation of the body's reactions, keeping a food diary.

## **14.5. Common Mistakes and How to Avoid Them**

1.  **Violation of the water regimen:** Drinking during or immediately after meals. This dilutes gastric juice and impairs digestion. **Solution:** strictly adhere to the rule "water 20-30 minutes BEFORE."
2.  **Evening fruits:** Consuming fruits after lunch leads to fermentation, bloating, and sleep disturbance. **Solution:** strictly limit fruits to the time before 12:00.
3.  **Insufficient heat treatment of eggs and meat:** Soft-boiled eggs or undercooked meat with blood do not meet the system's requirements for neutralizing potentially difficult-to-digest proteins. **Solution:** make prolonged boiling of eggs and complete bleeding of meat a mandatory ritual.
4.  **Experiments with prohibited vegetables:** "Just one tomato" or "a spoonful of mashed potatoes" can provoke a GI reaction, especially in the presence of hidden sensitivity to nightshades. **Solution:** consider the prohibition as therapeutic, not dietary, and strictly adhere to it.
5.  **Non-compliance with seasonality:** Eating the same way year-round deprives the system of its adaptive power. **Solution:** plan the menu focusing on local seasonal products.
## **14.6. Monitoring Progress: What Will Improve and When**

The patient must understand the expected effects and their timeframes to maintain motivation.

*   **1-2 weeks:** Normalization of stool, reduction of abdominal bloating, feeling of lightness after meals, improved sleep. Temporary weakness may be observed ("cleansing crisis").
*   **1 month:** Stabilization of energy levels throughout the day, reduction of morning edema, improvement in skin condition (reduction of breakouts), beginning of weight normalization.
*   **3 months:** Sustained improvement in digestion, significant reduction in the frequency and severity of allergic reactions (if present), normalization of blood pressure (if there was a tendency for it to be high), stable emotional state.
*   **6 months and beyond:** Deep restoration of metabolic processes, strengthening of immunity, improvement in blood markers (cholesterol, glucose), stable remission of chronic gastrointestinal diseases (gastritis, colitis). Eating according to the system becomes a natural, intuitive way of life.

**Clinical example:** Patient M., 45 years old, diagnosed with "irritable bowel syndrome with flatulence" and stage I hypertension. After 3 months of following the integrated system, he reported complete disappearance of IBS symptoms, normalization of blood pressure without adjustment of antihypertensive therapy, and a weight loss of 8 kg. According to the patient, the key factors were eliminating nightshades, practicing drinking water before meals, and maintaining a strict eating schedule.

# Appendix B. Quick Reference Guide to Prohibited and Allowed Products in the Tkemaladze-Gegeshidze System

The Tkemaladze-Gegeshidze system is a modification and development of Pevzner's principles, aimed at standardizing therapeutic nutrition in hospitals. It classifies products according to their degree of impact on gastric secretion and GI motility, allowing for quick formation of individual diets. Key recommendations of this system are provided below.

### Table 1. Prohibited — and What to Replace With

| Prohibited Product | Reason for Restriction | Recommended Replacement |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Rich meat/fish/mushroom broths** | Strong stimulants of gastric secretion, extractives. | Secondary broths (discard first water), vegetable broths, soups on weak broth. |
| **Fried dishes (meat, fish, potatoes)** | Contain fat oxidation products, irritate mucous membranes, enhance secretion. | Boiled, steamed, baked (without crust) dishes. |
| **Smoked meats, sausages** | High content of salt, fat, extractives, preservatives. | Boiled meat in pieces, steamed cutlets, soufflé from lean meat or fish. |
| **Kefir, yogurt, sour cream, cottage cheese** | Casein — a potential inflammation trigger; yeast cultures. | *Sulguni* or Imeretian cheese (aged 40+ days). For "freshness" — herbal infusions, pickled cucumbers. |
| **Buckwheat** | Empirical observation: worsening in patients with biliary tract diseases. | Millet (*gomi*), pearl barley, spelt. |
| **Potatoes** | High glycemic index, oxalates. | Pumpkin, zucchini, celery root, cauliflower (puree). |
| **Tomatoes** | Alpha-tomatine, irritation of bile ducts. | *Tklapi* (sour plum paste), *tkemali* without pepper, pomegranate juice — for acidity. Beets, paprika — for color. |
| **Olive oil** | Irritation of bile ducts; oxidation when heated. | Refined sunflower oil (for dressing), ghee/butter (for frying). |
| **Eggplant, spinach, sorrel** | High concentration of oxalates. | Zucchini, cauliflower, broccoli, green beans. |
| **Pork, duck, goose** | High fat content, extractives. | Veal, turkey, rabbit (after bleeding). |
| **Bread with thermophilic yeast** | Rapid fermentation, incomplete gluten breakdown. | Bread on homemade/wine sourdough or with long fermentation (12–24 hours). |
| **Corn, semolina** | Corn — high GI, incomplete protein. Semolina — refined starch without nutritional value. | Spelt, millet, pearl barley, rice porridge on water. |
